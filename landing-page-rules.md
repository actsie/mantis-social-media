# Landing Page Rules — Beauty & Salon Businesses

Patterns observed across real salon/spa/beauty websites during outreach research.
Use these as a checklist when building or critiquing landing pages.

Last updated: 2026-02-27

---

## 🔴 Most Common Problems (seen repeatedly)

### 1. Best content buried too deep
The most credibility-building thing on the page is almost always near the bottom — after booth rentals, hiring sections, and footer links. First-time visitors rarely scroll that far.

- Crystal-Eyez: Celebrity gallery (Vivica A Fox, K.Michelle, Kelly Price, Tasha Cobbs) buried below the fold after booth rental info
- Saltmara: Membership program with rollover credits + gifting in a plain bullet list at the bottom
- RYU Nail Bar: Gallery section too weak/low on page relative to where people are deciding to book
- The Nail Hall: No gallery section at all — nowhere for the work to speak for itself near the top

**Rule:** Put your single strongest trust signal in the first screen. Celebrity clients, years in business, awards, before/after work — whatever it is, lead with it.

---

### 2. CTAs that blend in or disappear
The booking/contact action is either missing, visually weak, or buried behind other content.

- LUX SF2: Main CTA in the navbar blends in visually, easy to miss when someone is moving fast
- Shiva Beauty: Homepage is image-heavy but the main action (book/call/what to do next) doesn't stand out clearly
- Saltmara: Membership CTA doesn't exist — the program is described but there's no button, no next step
- Salon Sevhn (GlossGenius page): Booking exists but no context around it — just a wall of service names

**Rule:** One clear primary CTA per screen. It should visually stand out, not compete with other elements. The visitor should never have to hunt for what to do next.

---

### 3. Social proof is present but presented badly
Reviews exist. Testimonials exist. They just don't land where they should.

- Crystal-Eyez: "The Streets are Talking" section on the homepage contains a raw Google search URL instead of formatted testimonials — looks unfinished
- The Muse SF: Positive reviews about the owner following up personally never surface on the page
- Most businesses: Google/Yelp review highlights that are genuinely compelling never make it onto the website
- Crystal-Eyez: Bridal testimonials from The Knot/WeddingWire shown as 2 text blurbs — not enough for a business where weddings are 30/133 reviews

**Rule:** Pull your best 2-3 review quotes onto the page. Real names, specific details, ideally with photos. Generic "great service!" doesn't do anything. "Kat is a goddess hair whisperer" does.

---

### 4. Named stylists/technicians not featured
Repeat clients almost always come back for a specific person. But the website shows nothing about who works there.

- Salon Sevhn: Karla, Lawanda, Kat each have devoted repeat clients — none of them are on the page
- Saltmara: Magdalena (mentioned in multiple recent reviews), Ruth (4 Yelp reviews), Regan — zero presence on the website
- JJ's Threading: Shipra mentioned repeatedly by name for her patience — not featured anywhere
- Crystal-Eyez: Bisi is the founder and most-mentioned artist but her bio is near the bottom

**Rule:** If clients are mentioning specific people by name in reviews, those people are your strongest retention asset. Feature them: photo, name, specialty. Give new clients a reason to ask for them.

---

### 5. Services listed without context
Menus that tell you what but not why, or what's included.

- The Muse SF: Review mentioned confusion about what was included after checking the website — expectation gap
- Salon Sevhn: 50 services on GlossGenius with no framing — a new client has no idea where to start
- Shiva Beauty: Service menu hard to scan, especially for someone trying to make a quick decision

**Rule:** Group services clearly. Include a brief "what's this?" for anything non-obvious. Add callouts for the most popular or most unique services. Help a new client land on the right thing fast.

---

### 6. Too much happening on first load
Popups, cookie banners, promotional overlays — all competing with the actual page before the visitor has seen anything.

- JJ's Threading Salon: First view has promo popup + cookie banner + animated hearts simultaneously — a new visitor has to dismiss 3 things before they can see what the salon offers

**Rule:** First impression is one job. Don't make the visitor work before they've seen anything. If you have a promo, show it after they've had 10 seconds to see the page.

---

### 7. Brand identity mismatch
What a client finds on Google doesn't match what they land on.

- Salon Sevhn: Google listing says "Salon Sevhn" — the GlossGenius page header says "SEVHN CORPORATION" (the legal entity name)

**Rule:** The name, logo, and tone on your website should match exactly what's on your Google listing, Yelp, and Instagram. If someone finds you by one name, that name should be the first thing they see.

---

### 8. Revenue driver not reflected in hero
The main money-maker for the business is often buried or deprioritized on the page.

- Crystal-Eyez: Weddings are mentioned in 30 of 133 Google reviews and the Yelp MOG/MOB cluster — but the homepage hero says "Need a Flawless Makeover?" There's a bridal page but it's not the entry point
- Saltmara: Membership program is their best retention tool but it's a footnote

**Rule:** Whatever drives the most revenue or repeat business should be the hero section, or at minimum the second thing a visitor sees. Don't hide your best offer.

---

### 9. Differentiators live in posts, not on the page
The thing that makes a business genuinely interesting is often only in a Google post or Instagram story — it never makes it to the website.

- Saltmara: TheraFace LED mask add-on for $10 (Feb 2026 owner post) — a real differentiator that sets them apart from every other nail salon in the area — not on the website

**Rule:** If you're posting about it, it should be on your page. A Google post has a 24-hour lifespan. A website section lasts forever.

---

### 10. 10 years+ of tenure undersold
Long-running businesses often don't lead with how long they've been around, even though it's one of the strongest trust signals available.

- Crystal-Eyez: 18 years in business (Jan 2026 anniversary post) — not visible on the homepage
- JJ's Threading: Long-established, loyal regulars, strong word of mouth — the website doesn't telegraph longevity

**Rule:** If you've been open 5+ years, say it loudly and early. "18 years in business" in the first screen does more trust-building than most testimonials.

---

## ✅ What Works (positive patterns to replicate)

- **Specific named services with personality** (Saltmara's Dazzle Dry, Crystal-Eyez's airbrush makeup) — gives new clients something to picture and ask for by name
- **Owner bio near the top** (Crystal-Eyez has Bisi's credentials well-structured, even if too low on page) — founder-led salons benefit from leading with the founder's story
- **Membership / loyalty programs** (Saltmara) — rollover credits + gifting is genuinely rare. When featured properly, it converts walk-ins to recurring clients
- **Responding to every review** — both Crystal-Eyez and Saltmara respond to Google reviews warmly and promptly. Signals an active, caring business to anyone reading before booking
- **"Book online" button in Google Maps** — all three have it. This is table stakes now. If you don't have it, you're losing bookings

---

## 📐 Page Structure That Converts (for beauty/salon)

Based on what's missing across the businesses researched:

```
1. Hero — Name + clearest value prop + primary CTA (book now)
2. Trust signal — Years in business / celebrity clients / awards / press
3. Services — Top 3-5, grouped clearly, with brief "why this" context
4. Team — Named stylists/techs with photos and specialties
5. Work gallery — Real photos, recent, let the work speak
6. Social proof — 2-3 specific review quotes (names, details, not generic)
7. Membership / offers — If they have one, this is where it converts
8. Contact + booking — Address, hours, phone, book button again
```
